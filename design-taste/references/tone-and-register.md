# Tone and Register

Two orthogonal calibrations. **Register** is the surface's job — it sets
the boldness *budget*. **Tone** is where the design currently sits against
that budget — too quiet, right, or too loud. Most bad feedback loops come
from adjusting tone without knowing the register: making a dashboard
"pop", making a landing page "clean" into anonymity.

## The four registers

- **Marketing / landing** — the surface *is* the brand; a visitor spends
  seconds. Spend the signature here: characterful display type, colour
  commitment, the one orchestrated motion moment. High boldness budget,
  still one signature.
- **Product app / tool** — someone lives here for hours a day.
  Personality moves into the details: precise spacing, considered empty
  states, quiet micro-feedback, excellent defaults. System type stacks
  and restrained palettes are often the *right* choice. Decoration that
  costs daily-use clarity is a defect.
- **Docs / long-form** — the reading experience is the design. Measure,
  rhythm, hierarchy and navigation carry everything; decoration budget is
  near zero. A beautiful docs site is beautiful typography.
- **Dashboard / data-heavy** — data is the hero and chrome recedes.
  Colour is reserved for *meaning* (state, series, alerts), so the brand
  must live in neutrals, type and spacing. Motion near zero; a dancing
  dashboard is an untrusted dashboard.

The tells are banned in every register. Register never re-admits a banned
pattern; it only sets how much boldness the surface can carry.

## Bolder — commitment, not effects

When a design is timid, the reflex is to add: gradients, glows, glass,
more colour. That is the opposite of bold. **If every element gets
louder, the composition gets flatter** — impact is contrast, not volume.

The levers, in order:

1. **Type dominance** — make the most important text meaningfully more
   dominant and the supporting text *quieter*; widen the gap, don't
   raise the floor.
2. **Colour decisiveness** — use the existing palette harder (a step up
   the commitment spectrum, `colour.md`) before inventing new colours.
3. **Proportion and asymmetry** — scale jumps, density contrast, an
   off-centre composition; let one element be genuinely large.
4. **Surface conviction** — apply the existing border/shadow/radius rules
   more deliberately; do not layer new decoration.
5. **Scale the signature** — one element, larger and braver; not a second
   signature.

If the result looks like every other "bold" AI design (dark, neon, glass),
it failed — that is the costume, not boldness.

## Quieter — restraint, not erasure

When a design shouts, the reflex is to strip everything — which collapses
it into the generic. **Quiet without intent is just generic.** The point
of view survives; the volume drops.

The levers:

1. **Saturation** — pull accents back a step (roughly 70–85% of full);
   keep the hues.
2. **Contrast allocation** — keep high contrast only where it matters
   most; soften it everywhere else.
3. **Competing weights** — one dominant element per view; demote the
   rest through weight and colour, not deletion.
4. **Motion** — dramatic effects become gentle feedback within the same
   timing system.
5. **Decorative layers** — remove gradients, patterns and shadows that
   carry no meaning; keep the one that is the signature.
6. **Scale jumps** — compress extreme size ratios one notch.

Quiet ≠ greyscale: colour remains, deployed strategically. Affordances
must stay obvious — subtlety never gets to make controls ambiguous
(`ux-design` owns that floor).

## The tone self-check

1. Name the register before touching anything. Does the polish budget
   match it?
2. Bolder request: did anything new get invented (colour, effect, font)?
   Prefer harder use of what exists.
3. Quieter request: is the signature still identifiable? If not, you
   erased the design instead of calming it.
4. Either direction: is the change contrast reallocation, or global
   volume? Global volume is the wrong lever in both directions.
