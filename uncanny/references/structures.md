# Structures and rhythm patterns

Each pattern comes with a fix **and its overcorrection trap**: the wrong fix that produces a different tell. Both columns matter.

## 1. Contrast-reframe (hard tell)

| Variant | Example |
|---|---|
| Negation drum-roll | "It's not just a tool. It's a platform." |
| Question pivot | "The question isn't whether X. It's when." |
| Reversal telegraph | "Not because it's cheap. Because it works." |
| Transformation arc | "X stops being a cost and starts being an asset." |
| Additive hedge | "not only X but also Y" |

**Fix:** state the positive claim. "It's a platform." If the contrast is genuinely informative (a common misconception worth correcting), name the misconception as a claim, not a rhythm: "Buyers assume it's a point tool; the licence covers the full platform."

**Overcorrection trap:** deleting every "not" and "but" from the text. Genuine contrast between two real ideas, stated once, is how argument works. The tell is negation-as-suspense, not negation.

## 2. Negative listing (hard tell)

"It wasn't the budget. It wasn't the timeline. It was the sponsor." A striptease. **Fix:** "The sponsor was the problem." If the ruled-out candidates matter (they usually don't), one clause: "and not budget or timeline, as the review first assumed".

**Trap:** collapsing to a fragment ("The sponsor. That was it."), which swaps a striptease for a mic-drop, which is pattern 5.

## 3. Tailing participle clauses (hard tell)

Sentence + comma + present participle asserting significance: "The system processed 4M records in the first quarter, **highlighting the scalability of the architecture**." The tail claims what the sentence should demonstrate.

Watch for: highlighting, underscoring, showcasing, demonstrating, reflecting, emphasising, signalling, cementing, solidifying, ensuring, marking, positioning.

**Fix:** delete the tail, or promote its claim to a sentence with evidence: "The architecture scaled linearly to 4M records with no configuration change."

**Trap:** converting the tail into a standalone vague declarative ("This highlights scalability."). Same claim, same emptiness, new punctuation.

## 4. Rule-of-three saturation (soft tell, density)

Triples are legitimate rhetoric. AI triples adjectives ("fast, reliable, and secure"), examples ("teams, departments, and organisations"), and clause rhythms, in every sentence, giving superficial analysis the shape of comprehensiveness.

**Density test:** three consecutive sentences or bullets each containing a triple → break at least one. Across a page, triples should be occasional, not the default list length.

**Fix:** use the number of items the content has. Two. Four. One, expanded properly.

**Trap:** a "two items only" rule. Uniform pairs are as metronomic as uniform triples.

## 5. Dramatic fragmentation / staccato (hard tell, and the signature overcorrection)

"Speed. That's it. That's the product." / "This changes something. Everything."

Manufactured profundity. **This is also what naive anti-slop editing produces**: chopping normal sentences into fragments to seem direct. A text where every sentence is under ten words is as detectable as one full of "delve".

**Fix:** complete sentences with varied length. Fragments are permitted rarely, deliberately, in conversational register only.

## 6. False agency (hard tell, register-dependent)

Inanimate things doing human verbs to avoid naming an actor: "the decision emerged", "the data tells us", "the culture shifted", "the roadmap demands". **Fix:** name who acted. "The board decided." "We read the data as X."

**Register caveat:** formal documents legitimately use institutional agents ("the Department requires", "the contract provides for") and some conventional metonymy ("the evidence suggests"). The tell is *evasion*: agency assigned to abstractions precisely where a real actor is being hidden. See registers.md.

## 7. Rhetorical setups (hard tell)

"What if I told you…", "Think about it:", "Here's what I mean:", question asked and answered in the next clause. **Fix:** make the point. A question is only worth asking if it stays open longer than one sentence.

**Trap:** banning all questions. A genuine open question, or a section heading framed as the question the section answers, is fine.

## 8. Passive voice (soft tell, register-dependent)

Passive that hides a knowable actor is a tell and a substance problem: "mistakes were made", "it was decided". **Fix:** name the actor.

Passive that foregrounds the object because the actor is unknown, irrelevant, or the object is the topic is correct English: "The service was decommissioned in 2024." Technical and formal registers use more passive legitimately.

**Trap:** mechanical active-voice conversion that inserts fake agents ("The team decommissioned the service" when you don't know who did).

## 9. Wh-/So-/Look- openers (soft tell)

"What makes this hard is…" as the default sentence engine, paragraphs opening with "So,". Both are crutches. **Fix:** lead with the subject or the specific constraint. **Trap:** a total ban; an occasional "What changed in v2 is…" is normal prose.

## 10. Uniform paragraph architecture (soft tell, the shape of the page)

Every paragraph 3–4 sentences; every paragraph ends with a neat concluding bow; every section the same length; every heading followed by exactly one intro sentence and a bullet list. The page has a *texture* that reads machine-made before anyone reads a word.

**Fix:** vary paragraph length with the content's weight. Let one idea take a page and another take a line. End some paragraphs mid-thought where the next picks up. Use a list only when items are genuinely parallel and scannable.

## 11. Em dashes (hard tell, banned outright)

The em dash is a special case: legitimate punctuation that has become the single most recognised AI tell. Readers do not count density; they flag the character at a glance, in any register, and downgrade the text before reading the sentence. Since a comma, colon, parentheses, or full stop covers every use, the cost of a total ban is near zero and the perception benefit is large. This is a ban on perception grounds, not grammar grounds.

**Scope:** all em dashes, and spaced parenthetical dashes of any width ("the result – which surprised us – held"). En dashes survive only in numeric ranges (2019–2024, pp. 40–70), which nobody reads as AI.

**Fix by function:**

| The dash was doing | Replace with |
|---|---|
| Introducing an elaboration or example | colon |
| Parenthetical aside | parentheses, or paired commas if short |
| Joining two related independent clauses | semicolon, or split into two sentences |
| Punched-up contrast ("not X — but Y") | this is also pattern 1; state Y directly |
| Trailing afterthought | full stop, then a sentence that earns its place or gets cut |

**Trap:** mechanical substitution. Swapping every dash for a comma manufactures comma splices; swapping every dash for a colon produces a page of colons, which is its own texture. Restructure the worst sentences rather than re-punctuating them, and vary the replacements across the page.

## 12. False ranges (hard tell)

"from X to Y" where X and Y aren't endpoints of a scale: "from onboarding to analytics to culture". That's a list wearing a range costume. **Fix:** "onboarding, analytics, and culture" (if genuinely three) or name the actual range ("from 10 to 40,000 users").

## 13. Vague attribution (hard tell)

"Experts agree", "studies show", "it is widely recognised" with nothing citable behind it. **Fix:** name the source ("the 2025 OGP guidance", "Gartner's 2026 MQ") or convert to an owned claim ("in our experience across five Irish public-sector deployments"). If neither exists, the claim goes.

## 14. Significance inflation / puffery (hard tell)

"stands as a testament to", "plays a vital role in shaping", "a rich heritage", "boasts a wide array". Adjectival importance with no evidence. **Fix:** the evidence itself. "Processed 1.2M applications since 2019" beats "plays a pivotal role in national service delivery".
