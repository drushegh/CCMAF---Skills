# Writing, review & dissemination

Producing the output, pressure-testing it, and communicating it to the right
audience. Condensed from `imbad0202/academic-research-skills` (reviewer
simulation), `ngtiendong` (claim verification) and standard academic practice.

## Academic writing

- **IMRaD** for empirical papers: **I**ntroduction (question, why it matters,
  contribution), **M**ethods (reproducible — see `methods-and-reproducibility.md`),
  **R**esults (findings only, no interpretation), **D**iscussion (interpretation,
  limitations, implications). A review uses Introduction / Methods / Results
  (themes) / Discussion / Limitations / Conclusion.
- **Structured abstract** (≤~250 words): background · objective · methods ·
  results · conclusion. It is the most-read part and must stand alone — it
  contains **no claim that isn't in the body**.
- **Register:** precise and concise; hedge to match the confidence label
  (`source-appraisal-and-integrity.md`) — neither overclaiming nor mushy;
  active voice where it aids clarity; define terms; signpost. Spelling/format per
  the target venue.
- **Synthesise, don't recite** (`synthesis-and-review.md`); lead with the answer
  then the support; keep every sentence traceable to evidence or labelled as
  interpretation/hypothesis.

## Reviewer simulation (before you submit or share)

Pressure-test by reviewing from **non-overlapping lenses** — assemble a panel
rather than one generic pass:

| Reviewer | Lens |
|---|---|
| Editor-in-Chief | Scope/fit, significance, overall decision |
| Methodology | Design, validity, statistics, reproducibility |
| Domain | Correctness, prior-work coverage, novelty vs the field |
| Cross-disciplinary | Clarity, accessibility, transfer beyond the niche |
| Devil's advocate | Strongest counter-argument, logical fallacies, failure modes |

Produce per-lens comments → an **editorial decision** (accept / minor / major /
reject) → a **prioritised revision roadmap**. After revising, run a
**re-review** pass that checks each change actually addresses its comment. This
is structured self-critique — the human keeps final judgement; never rubber-stamp
by skipping the devil's advocate.

## Claim-verification pass (gate before delivery)

Run the claim gate: every claim traceable; **no fabricated citation**; no claim
stronger than its evidence; comparative claims have baselines; the reference list
verified (`citations-and-referencing.md`). Evidence and interpretation stay
visibly separate.

## Dissemination — match the artifact to the audience

- **Structured / graphical abstract** — the work in one screen.
- **Conference poster** — one narrative, generous whitespace, a few high-impact
  figures, legible at ~1.5 m; not a wall of text.
- **Slides** — one idea per slide, signpost, build to the contribution; the
  speaker carries the detail.
- **Plain-language / policy summary** — no jargon, lead with the "so what",
  and state confidence and limitations honestly. Most relevant for
  decision-maker, tender and advisory audiences (→ your docx/pptx tooling
  for the formatted artifact).

## Pitfalls

- Abstract or summary claims not supported in the body.
- Interpretation smuggled into the results; limitations omitted.
- Reviewer simulation used as a rubber stamp (devil's advocate skipped).
- Dissemination that overclaims beyond what the evidence supports.
